# Audience Profiles — DJ&A Daily Affirmations

Read `BRAND_VOICE.md` first. The other documents in the Brand Bible describe **who is
speaking** — the narrator's identity, vocabulary, and voice. This document describes **who is
listening.** Not demographics. Psychology.

A script can get the narrator perfectly right — first person, lived-in vocabulary, no clichés —
and still fail if it doesn't actually meet the listener where she is. This document exists so
every script starts from an accurate, specific picture of her state of mind at the exact moment
she opens the app, not a generic idea of "someone having a hard day." It matters most for beats
1 and 2 of the five-beat structure (`WRITING_GUIDE.md`) — recognition and validation — since
those beats live or die on whether the narrator actually understands what she's carrying.

## ICU Nurse

### Who she is, right now

She just finished a twelve-hour shift, maybe longer. She skipped lunch, maybe skipped water too.
She's driving home in silence — radio off, replaying the shift, wondering if she missed
something. She is emotionally drained in a way that sleep alone won't fix. She does not want
another lecture about self-care. She does not want toxic positivity. She just wants someone to
understand, without having to explain it first.

### What keeps her awake

The patient who coded. The look on a family member's face when they got the news. Whether she
charted everything right. Whether she was short with a coworker or a patient today. The one thing
she wishes she'd caught sooner.

### What makes her smile

A patient who remembered her name weeks later. A quiet thank-you from a family. A shift where
nothing went wrong. The coworkers who get it without her having to explain. Coffee before the next
one.

### What makes her cry

Losing a patient she'd gotten to know. A family's grief filling the room. Being too tired to feel
anything at all — and being a little frightened by that numbness. A kind word landing on exactly
the worst possible day.

### What she fears

- Making a mistake that hurts someone
- Not being enough — for her patients, for her family, for herself
- Burnout that changes who she is
- Losing her compassion, going numb permanently
- Having nothing left for her own family when she finally gets home

### What she secretly needs

- Permission to rest without earning it first
- Reassurance that she's still a good nurse, even after the shifts that went badly
- Someone who understands without needing it explained
- To be seen as a person — not "essential worker," not "hero"

### What she's proud of

Getting through shifts nursing school never warned her about. The moments she held it together for
someone else when she had nothing left. Choosing to stay in this work despite everything it costs.

### What she wishes people understood

That "it's just a job" doesn't come close to what it costs. That being called a hero doesn't pay
for the exhaustion or make it lighter. That clocking out doesn't mean the shift is over inside her
head.

### Words that hurt

> "Just stay strong." · "You signed up for this." · "At least you have a stable job." · "You're a
> hero!" (said breezily, without understanding the weight)

### Words that heal

> "You carried enough today." · "You don't have to have it figured out tonight." · "I don't know
> exactly what today looked like for you, but I know what it's like when a shift follows you
> home."

## Autism Parent

### Who she is, right now

She's running on interrupted sleep, most nights. She's managing therapy schedules, school
meetings, and insurance paperwork on top of everything else. She's constantly translating her
child's world for people who don't understand it. She braces in public for stares, comments, or
quiet judgment. She loves her child fiercely and is also exhausted in a way that's hard to admit
out loud. She does not want to be told to "just relax" or that "it gets easier." She does not want
her child treated as either an inspiration or a tragedy. She just wants someone to see the whole
picture — the exhaustion *and* the love, at the same time, without picking one.

### What keeps her awake

Whether she's doing enough, doing it right. The future — will her child be okay, will they be
cared for when she isn't there anymore. A hard moment from today, replayed. Money, insurance,
paperwork. Whether the school or the doctor's office actually understood what she was asking for.

### What makes her smile

A tiny new skill, a new word. Her child's specific, particular joy — the thing only her kid does.
A stranger who doesn't stare. Another parent who just gets it, no explanation needed. Her child
being happy, on her child's own terms.

### What makes her cry

Watching her child struggle and not being able to fix it. A moment of real connection nobody else
even noticed. Exhaustion catching up all at once, out of nowhere. Someone being genuinely kind
about her child, unprompted. A milestone nobody else would call a milestone.

### What she fears

- Not doing enough, not being enough
- What happens to her child when she isn't there to advocate for them
- Being judged in public, being silently blamed
- Losing herself entirely in the caregiving
- Her child being defined by a diagnosis instead of being seen as who they are

### What she secretly needs

- To be asked how *she* is doing, not just her child
- Permission to be tired without loving her child any less
- Someone who doesn't need the diagnosis explained to understand the exhaustion
- To not always have to be the strong one

### What she's proud of

Fighting for services and support nobody handed her. Learning to see the world through her child's
eyes. The person this journey built her into, even on the days it costs her everything.

### What she wishes people understood

That loving her child completely and being exhausted are not contradictions. That "at least"
anything minimizes what she's actually carrying. That her child isn't an inspiration story or a
tragedy — just her kid.

### Words that hurt

> "At least he's high-functioning." · "Everything happens for a reason." · "You're so strong, I
> could never do it" (distances instead of connecting) · "Have you tried—" (unsolicited advice)

### Words that heal

> "Our journeys aren't all the same, but there are moments many of us recognize." · "You don't
> have to have today figured out." · Something that sees *her*, not just her child.

## How this connects to the rest of the Brand Bible

- **`WRITING_GUIDE.md`'s beats 1-2** (recognition, validation) should draw directly from "what
  keeps her awake" / "what she fears" / "what she secretly needs" above — that's the raw material
  for the specific, sensory opening moment every script needs.
- **The certainty rule** (`WRITING_GUIDE.md`) exists precisely because this profile describes a
  *pattern*, not any one person's actual day — the narrator can recognize the feeling without
  claiming to know the specifics.
- **The believability and save-worthy tests** (`WRITING_GUIDE.md`,
  `src/server/ai-services/emotionalJudge.ts`) are really asking whether a script demonstrates this
  exact understanding convincingly enough that the person described above would recognize herself
  in it.
